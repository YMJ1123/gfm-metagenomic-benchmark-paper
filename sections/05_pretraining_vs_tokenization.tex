\section{Pre-training versus tokenization}
\label{sec:decomp}

We now separate the two factors that a data-scaling curve alone cannot
distinguish: the value of pre-training and the value of tokenization. Each is
isolated by changing exactly one variable and comparing like with like
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}).

\subsection{At matched tokenization, pre-training helps}

Holding tokenization fixed at non-overlapping 6-mer and data at 50M reads, we
compare models. The 498M-parameter pre-trained NT-v2 reaches 67.07\% genus
Top-1, versus 47.00\% for a MetaTransformer trained from scratch on the identical
6-mer input, and 53.88\% for a single-layer randomly initialized transformer
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}, left). At a fixed tokenizer, then, a large
pre-trained backbone is worth roughly $+13$ to $+20$ pp over from-scratch models:
accuracy that no amount of extra 6-mer data recovers. This is the strongest case
for GFMs in metagenomics---the representation learned during pre-training
transfers even though the corpus is not microbial. Because backbone architecture
and capacity also differ across these systems, we read this as the benefit of a
pre-trained large backbone rather than an isolated pre-training term. The benefit
is not backbone-specific: across pre-trained GFMs fine-tuned identically at 5M
reads, NT-v2 (63.1\%) leads DNABERT (61.8\%) and DNABERT-2 (58.9\%), yet all
three stay far below the long-$k$ ceiling---their (sub-)6-mer-scale tokenizers,
not the choice of backbone, are the limiting factor (Supplementary).

\subsection{Tokenization is the larger lever}

The tokenizer, not the backbone, sets the ceiling. We isolate its effect
\emph{within} the MetaTransformer architecture---trained from scratch, so nothing
but the tokenizer changes. With a stride-1 overlapping tokenizer, moving from
$k=6$ to $k=13$ raises genus Top-1 from 48.87\% to 87.42\%: a $+38.6$ pp swing
from tokenization alone, on identical data and architecture
(Figure~\ref{fig:ablation}, right; Table~\ref{tab:tok}). We report this
comparison within MetaTransformer because NT-v2's tokenizer is fixed at
non-overlapping 6-mer by its pre-trained vocabulary and cannot be re-tokenized to
longer $k$; its 6-mer result (67.07\%) is the matched-tokenizer reference, and
notably sits below even the from-scratch 13-mer model. Changing the tokenizer
thus buys more than changing from a random network to a large pre-trained one.

Critically, tokenization also governs \emph{scalability}. As data grows from
50M to 250M, the overlapping-13-mer model improves by $+11$ pp
($87.4\%\!\rightarrow\!98.7\%$) while every 6-mer model is flat ($+0.22$ pp for
NT-v2; Section~\ref{sec:scaling}). A tokenizer that preserves enough local signal
both raises the ceiling and keeps the model in the regime where more data helps.

The practical implication is blunt, and we return to it in the recommendations
(Section~\ref{sec:recommendations}): a pre-trained backbone helps, but if its
tokenizer is fixed at non-overlapping 6-mer---as it is for most current
GFMs---neither more pre-training nor more data lifts the ceiling. For this task
the tokenizer, not backbone size, is the first property to check when choosing or
building a GFM.

\subsection{The ceiling is representational, not disguised lookup}
\label{sec:lookup}

Two objections to this picture deserve a direct answer; we summarise here and
give the full analysis in the Supplementary Material. First, the 6-mer model is
not merely under-trained: it cannot even \emph{fit} its training set past
$\sim$66\% (250M warm-start: 65.8\% train vs 66.6\% validation), whereas the
13-mer model fits training to 98.9\%. When a model cannot memorize the data, the
bottleneck is the input representation, not capacity or optimization---the
non-overlapping 6-mer tokens simply do not carry enough separable signal for a
150\,bp read. Second, the 13-mer result is not disguised exact-match lookup: a
Kraken-style unique-key lookup scores only 0.72\% (the $4^{13}$ 13-mer space is
saturated by the reference, so test 13-mers are almost all ``seen'' but only
$\sim$0.4\% map to a single genus), a multinomial $k$-mer naive Bayes reaches
74.9\%, and the neural network adds a further $+13$ pp---learned high-dimensional
composition, not memorization. Applying the same naive Bayes to 6-mer composition
yields only 25.9\%, isolating $k$-mer \emph{length} as the decisive variable, and
the non-neural 13-mer naive Bayes (74.9\%) already exceeds the 498M-parameter
NT-v2 on 6-mer (67\%). We are explicit about the accompanying caveat: because the
reference genomes are nearly fully covered by the test organisms, the 98.7\%
figure characterizes \emph{closed-set} classification against known genomes;
novel-genome generalization is a separate question we flag as the primary
limitation (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}).
